The Electric Newspaper was an Australian online newspaper published in Adelaide, South Australia, from February to July 1998. Published at the web address www.electric.on.net and hosted on a Unix server provided by Adelaide internet service provider Internode, it was among the first independent, journalist-led online-only daily newspapers in Australia, predating the widespread adoption of the internet by Australian households.

Produced by web design studio Virtual Artists Pty Ltd of Rundle Street, Adelaide, and edited by veteran broadcast journalist Hendrik Gout, the paper published 711 articles across eleven sections — including news, essays, arts, film reviews, sport, food, and holidays — written by a team of working journalists, academics, politicians, and critics. It operated for seven months before ceasing publication on 31 July 1998.

The paper's coverage was focused on South Australian affairs, and its seven-month lifespan coincided with one of the most turbulent periods in recent SA political history: the ETSA privatisation controversy and the parliamentary scandal surrounding Deputy Premier Graham Ingerson. The complete archive was preserved on tape and restored in 2024.

History

Founding

The Electric Newspaper was conceived and produced by Virtual Artists Pty Ltd, a web design studio on Rundle Street, Adelaide, which had established itself as one of South Australia's leading commercial web agencies in the mid-1990s. The studio had developed significant technical expertise in web production and had an existing relationship with Adelaide-based internet service provider Internode, which provided server infrastructure and co-sponsorship.

The paper launched in early February 1998 under editor Hendrik Gout, a veteran broadcast journalist who had previously served as Executive Producer of the ABC's 7.30 Report and had covered the SA State Bank Royal Commission. Describing the paper's staff in an early edition, Gout wrote: "We're a bunch of journalists, production staff (read: web designers), and general all-round bad guys ... We're running a daily newspaper."[1]

The paper's timing coincided with a period of rapid internet uptake in Australia. By 1998, the internet had moved from university and corporate networks to mainstream household use, driven by commercial ISPs such as Adelaide-based Internode, which had been offering public internet services since 1995.[2] Existing Australian newspaper websites — including those of major metropolitan dailies — were at this time largely static replicas of their print editions, updated infrequently. The Electric Newspaper was designed from the outset as an online-first publication with no print equivalent.

Publication

The paper published multiple articles per day, six days a week, across eleven sections: News, Essays, Arts, Films, Books, Food & Drink, Sport, Holidays, Fringe, Letters, and The Colonel (a regular column). Over its seven-month run it published 711 articles. The news section alone comprised 314 articles.

The paper's launch in February 1998 coincided with the Adelaide Festival and Adelaide Fringe Festival, both of which received intensive coverage. Arts and drama journalist Elaine Moore filed multiple Fringe reviews per week throughout March 1998.

The most prominent running story of the paper's existence was the ETSA privatisation controversy. When Premier John Olsen announced on 17 February 1998 that his Liberal government would sell ETSA — reversing a specific election promise — the announcement triggered a political crisis that dominated South Australian public life for months. ETSA is mentioned in 54 news articles and 30 essays published by the paper; Premier Olsen appears in 83 of the paper's 314 news articles.

Closure

The Electric Newspaper published its final edition on 31 July 1998, seven months after launch. The closing note in the paper's news index read: "No time to say hello, see ya! The Electric Newspaper is seven months old this week. It's now been seven months of hard work."[3] No explanation for the cessation of publication was given in the archive. The paper's Unix server at Internode was subsequently archived as a tar file and preserved.

Content

News coverage

The news section was the paper's largest and most frequently updated, publishing 314 articles over seven months. Coverage focused almost exclusively on South Australian affairs: state parliament, the courts, local government, and economic policy. Chief reporter Hendrik Gout authored more than 36 news articles under his byline; Garry Quill filed political dispatches from Parliament House, Canberra; and reporter Krista Eleftheriou covered tourism, transport, and regulatory matters.

The ETSA privatisation

The proposed privatisation of the Electricity Trust of South Australia (ETSA) was the defining story of the paper's run. The Olsen Liberal government's February 1998 announcement reversed a commitment made during the 1997 state election campaign. The paper covered the subsequent legislative battles extensively, including the role of crossbenchers such as Nick Xenophon, who had been elected to the Legislative Council in 1997 as an independent No Pokies MLC — the first independent elected to the LC in sixty years — and whose support the government required to pass the ETSA bill.

The Ingerson affair

Deputy Premier Graham Ingerson was alleged to have telephoned the chairman of the SA Thoroughbred Racing Authority to influence an executive appointment, then denied having done so before an estimates committee. The Speaker of the House ruled that a prima facie case of misleading parliament existed, and a parliamentary privileges committee — the first in South Australian parliamentary history — was convened. The committee had powers equivalent to a royal commission. The Electric Newspaper filed 35 articles on the affair, with Gout's coverage characterised by access to unnamed government sources. The paper reported on Liberal ministers privately expressing doubt that Ingerson could survive in his role, under headlines including "Olsen sticks by Ingo like doggydoo sticks to shoes" and "Dead but not buried."[4]

Essays and opinion

The Essays section was distinctive in that it regularly published signed opinion pieces by sitting members of the South Australian Parliament, writing directly under their own bylines rather than through press releases or media intermediaries. Regular essay contributors included:

  • Sandra Kanck MLC (SA Democrats), 16 essays — on voluntary euthanasia, gaming machine policy, environmental matters, and the Riverlink development proposal;
  • Legh Davis, policy analyst, 19 essays — the paper's most prolific contributor overall, covering SA Labor Party politics, the state budget, and ETSA;
  • John Spoehr, 7 essays — economic and social policy;
  • John Rau LLB (SA Labor), 5 essays — later SA Attorney-General and Deputy Premier; described by Gout as the paper's in-house legal counsel;
  • Nick Xenophon MLC (No Pokies), essays on gambling policy;
  • Patrick Conlon MHA (SA Labor), 3 essays.

Gout described the essays section's purpose as ensuring readers understood what elected representatives were thinking: "They vote on legislation. It's time we all knew more about what individual Members of Parliament were thinking, and why they do what they do."[1]

Sport editor Phil Owens wrote rugby league coverage under his own name and motorsport coverage — principally the 500cc World Championship and Formula One — under the pseudonym "Piston Stoned." His rugby league coverage documented the Adelaide Rams' second and final NRL season in 1998, during which the team finished seventeenth, average home attendances fell by more than half, and the club was wound up at season's end — the only South Australian club ever to compete at the top level of Australian rugby league.[5]

Cartoonist and travel writer George Aldridge served dual roles: drawing cartoons for the paper and filing dispatches from overseas as what the paper billed as its "uniquely own foreign correspondent." His Holidays column included a "Postcard from Ireland" series.

Technical production

All pages of The Electric Newspaper were hand-coded in HTML by Chris Joyner and Ben Moretti of Virtual Artists. The paper used a two-column table layout — standard for the period — with a 140-pixel left column containing graphical navigation buttons and a 460-pixel right column for article content.

Navigation used a rollover button system — one of the more sophisticated interactive features available in 1998 — implemented in JavaScript by Dave Sag and Mark King of Virtual Artists in July 1997. The script, which swapped GIF images on mouse hover events using the document.images object, was credited in its source code as follows: "This code was written by Dave Sag (dave@va.com.au) July 1997 ... based on Mark King's original waxon/off script."[7]

The paper was hosted on a Unix server operated by Internode at the domain electric.on.net, reflecting Internode's .on.net domain namespace. Internode and Cobweb Internet Services were acknowledged as sponsors on every page of the paper. Design and visual identity, including the masthead logo, was created by Andy Petrucevics.

The paper's section header graphics were stored in a dedicated HEADERS/ directory, navigation buttons in a BUTTONS/ directory, and story photographs in dated subdirectories within each section folder. Story photography was sourced from a range of contributors; editorial illustrations were stored in an ILLUSTRA/ directory.

Historical context

In 1998, the Australian internet was undergoing rapid expansion. AARNet had allowed commercial ISPs to connect since 1994; by 1997–98 household internet penetration was growing quickly. Established newspapers — The Advertiser in Adelaide and the metropolitan dailies in other states — had online presences, but these were extensions of their print operations, not independent digital publications. The ABC had launched ABC Online, which recorded over two million weekly hits during the 1998 federal election campaign.[8]

Internode, one of the paper's infrastructure sponsors, had been founded in Adelaide in 1991 by Simon Hackett and Robyn Taylor, initially as a software company before pivoting to become an ISP in 1994–95. It was among the first commercial ISPs in South Australia and would later become one of Australia's largest.[9]

South Australia in early 1998 was in political turmoil. The Olsen Liberal government, re-elected in 1997 partly on a promise not to privatise ETSA, reversed that position within months. At the same time, a new generation of crossbench MLCs — including Nick Xenophon (No Pokies) and the three-seat Australian Democrats bloc led by Sandra Kanck — held the balance of power in the Legislative Council, making every significant legislative vote uncertain. The Electric Newspaper's news section served as a real-time chronicle of this period.

Legacy and archive

Following the paper's closure in July 1998, the content was preserved on Internode's Unix server as a tar archive. The complete file set — 1,711 files comprising 941 HTML article pages, 558 photographs, 205 GIF graphics, 5 video files, and 2 PDFs — remained dormant until 2024, when it was extracted and restored.

The restoration process revealed that the tar archive had truncated all filenames to eight characters, rendering every image reference in all 711 articles broken. Systematic analysis recovered 92% of broken references by mapping truncated filenames back to their originals. A further 269 absolute image URLs pointing to the defunct www.electric.on.net domain were rewritten as relative paths. The original article HTML was not modified; changes were limited to image path corrections, navigation link fixes, and the disabling — with full source preservation in code comments — of the 1997-era rollover button JavaScript, which was incompatible with contemporary browsers.

The restored archive is being prepared for submission to PANDORA, the National Library of Australia's archive of online Australian publications, and to Trove.
