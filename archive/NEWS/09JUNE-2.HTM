Tuesday June 9, 1998

More marine parks needed, says study
by Chris Warren

South Australia shares the Dunce's Cap with Victoria when it comes to Government efforts to safeguard and protect the marine environment.

That's the finding of the Worldwildlife Fund for Nature. It gave South Australia a 'D' rating, marginally ahead of our south-east neighbours.

South Australia would have been the pariah State without the proclamation of the Great Australian Bight Marine Park two years ago, when whales were given 'part-time' protection from commercial fishing operations and oil exploration.

Very few improvements have been made since.

Tony Flaherty, of the Marine and Coastal Community Network, says a network of 'no-take' marine reserves are needed around the S.A. coast.

Interstate research suggests areas of about seven kilometres of coast, set aside with no fishing and no mineral extraction, would be really good for fish stocks to recover," Mr Flaherty said.

South Australian commercial fishing interests strongly oppose marine protected areas, and have successfully lobbied the State Government to get exemptions inside the Great Australian Bight.

In Tasmania, commercial crayfish (rock lobster) divers have come up with a better approach.

"In Tasmania, recreational and commercial fishing groups got together to urge their own marine reserves as fish propogation areas," Mr Flaherty said.

In those no-take areas, crayfish numbers almost tripled - they increased by 260 per cent, compared with a paltry 12 per cent improvement outside marine reserves.

Mr Flaherty said Australia needed a network of marine reserves where fish numbers could improve - and the effect of that would be to see juveniles spreading around the entire coast of the continent.
