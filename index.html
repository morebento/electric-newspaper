What It Was

The Electric Newspaper was a daily online newspaper published in Adelaide, South Australia from February to July 1998. It was among the first independent, journalist-led online newspapers in Australia — published entirely on the web, with no print edition, at a moment when most Australians were still discovering the internet for the first time.

The paper ran news, essays, arts, film and book reviews, food, sport, holidays, and a letters page. At its peak it was publishing multiple stories a day, six days a week, written by working journalists and edited to professional standard.

Where It Came From

The paper grew out of the web design studio Virtual Artists on Rundle Street, Adelaide — one of the city's earliest commercial web agencies. The team already had the server, the skills, and the contacts in Adelaide's journalism community. The idea was to put them together and publish every day.

Virtual Artists had built a reputation for pushing what the web could do. The idea of a daily online newspaper felt like the logical next experiment: journalism at the speed of the web, unconstrained by print deadlines or proprietors.

Why It Matters

In 1998, Australian newspaper websites were mirrors of their print parents: slow to update, conservative in design, built by IT departments not journalists. The Electric Newspaper was something different — born digital, Adelaide-first, and written by people who cared deeply about South Australian politics, culture, and daily life.

It also captured, in real time, one of the most turbulent periods in recent SA political history: the Olsen government's surprise ETSA privatisation, the Ingerson parliamentary scandal, and the rise of a new generation of independent MLCs including Nick Xenophon.

Origin & Context

A newspaper born on Rundle Street

Virtual Artists was founded in Adelaide in the mid-1990s and quickly became one of South Australia's leading web design studios, working with corporate clients across the country and producing some of the most technically ambitious websites in Australia at the time. The Electric Newspaper was served from a Unix server at Internode — Adelaide's own ISP, and one of the country's most progressive early internet providers.

The internet reached Australian homes at scale between 1994 and 1997. Adelaide was served by two of the country's most progressive early ISPs: Internode, founded in 1991 by Simon Hackett and Robyn Taylor in Adelaide, which began offering commercial internet services in 1995; and Cobweb Internet Services. Both became sponsors and infrastructure partners of The Electric Newspaper, their logos appearing at the foot of every page.

The paper launched in early February 1998. Within weeks it had established a rhythm: news from the SA Parliament and the courts in the morning, essays and opinion from politicians and academics in the afternoon, reviews of Adelaide's arts, restaurants, and films throughout the week. The server address was www.electric.on.net, hosted on Internode's network.

Timeline

  • 1991–95 Internode founded in Adelaide (1991) by Simon Hackett and Robyn Taylor; begins commercial ISP services in 1995. Adelaide develops one of Australia's most active early internet communities.
  • Mid-1990s Virtual Artists established on Rundle Street, Adelaide, as one of SA's first commercial web design studios. Develops the rollover button JavaScript that would appear on every Electric Newspaper page.
  • Mar 1997 SA state election. The Olsen Liberal government is re-elected, promising not to privatise ETSA, the state-owned electricity utility. Nick Xenophon elected to the Legislative Council as an independent on a No Pokies platform — the first independent elected to the LC in 60 years.
  • 17 Feb 1998 Premier John Olsen announces plans to privatise ETSA — reversing his election promise. The announcement triggers a political crisis that will dominate SA news for the next six months, and becomes the defining story of The Electric Newspaper's run.
  • Feb 1998 The Electric Newspaper launches at www.electric.on.net. First articles cover the Adelaide Festival / Fringe season and breaking state political news. Adelaide's first online-only daily newspaper is live.
  • Mar 1998 The Adelaide Festival and Fringe Festival are in full swing; the paper covers both intensively. Elaine Moore files multiple reviews per week. The paper's arts, food, and film sections find their regular contributors and rhythms.
  • Apr–Jun 1998 The Ingerson affair: Deputy Premier Graham Ingerson is alleged to have misled parliament over a phone call about a racing authority appointment. The Speaker rules a prima facie case exists; a privileges committee — the first in SA parliamentary history — is convened. Hendrik Gout files 36+ articles on the unfolding crisis.
  • Jun 1998 The Adelaide Rams NRL team, mid-season, relocates from Adelaide Oval to Hindmarsh Stadium after a financial dispute with the SACA. Attendance collapses. Sport editor Phil Owens (also writing motorsport as “Piston Stoned”) chronicles their decline.
  • 31 Jul 1998 The Electric Newspaper publishes its final edition. The paper is seven months old. The farewell note in the index reads: “No time to say hello, see ya!” The Internode Unix server is archived to a tar file and sits dormant for 26 years.
  • 2024 The tar file is extracted and the archive restored. Broken image paths caused by 8-character filename truncation in the tar archive are mapped and fixed. 96% of original image references are recovered. The archive is prepared for submission to PANDORA / TROVE.

The Stories That Defined It

The ETSA Saga — 84 articles

No story ran longer or deeper than the Olsen government's decision to privatise ETSA — the Electricity Trust of South Australia. Announced in February 1998, it broke a direct election promise made the previous year. Opponents argued the sale would cost the government more in lost dividends than it would raise. The scandal ran through the paper's entire lifespan: 54 news articles and 30 essays mention ETSA by name. Premier Olsen appears in 83 news articles — nearly one in three.

The Ingerson Affair — 35 articles

Deputy Premier Graham Ingerson was alleged to have telephoned the chairman of the SA Thoroughbred Racing Authority to influence the appointment of a chief executive, then denied doing so before an estimates committee. The Speaker ruled a prima facie case of misleading parliament existed. The resulting privileges committee was the first in SA parliamentary history — a body with the powers of a royal commission. Gout chronicled it in granular detail, including unnamed Liberal ministers openly wondering if Ingerson would survive.

The Adelaide Fringe 1998

The paper launched almost simultaneously with the 1998 Adelaide Festival and Fringe season, and covered both intensively through March. Elaine Moore's Fringe reviews capture a moment when the Festival was still finding its footing as the mass-market event it would later become.

The Rams' Last Season

The Adelaide Rams' 1998 NRL season — their second and last — became a slow-motion tragedy Phil Owens recorded week by week. The team lost nine of their first ten games, the coach was sacked, they abandoned Adelaide Oval for Hindmarsh Stadium, and attendance fell by more than half. The club folded at season's end, remaining the only South Australian team ever to play top-flight rugby league.

About This Archive

This archive was restored in 2024 from the original Unix tar file extracted from the Solaris server that hosted www.electric.on.net. The tar process had truncated all filenames to eight characters, breaking every image reference in the 711 HTML articles. The restoration process mapped 92% of broken references back to their original files through systematic pattern matching, rewrote 269 absolute URLs to relative paths, fixed cross-browser JavaScript compatibility in 923 files, and built section index pages for every content area.

The article text and body HTML is entirely original and unmodified. The only changes to article files are: image path corrections, navigation link fixes, and the disabling (with preservation in comments) of a Netscape 3-era rollover button script.

Technical note: Original HTML hand-coded by Chris Joyner and Ben Moretti, 1998. Rollover button JavaScript by Dave Sag and Mark King, Virtual Artists Pty Ltd, July 1997. Hosted on Internode (on.net) and Cobweb Internet Services. Internet service provider logos retained at the foot of each article page as they appeared in 1998. Archive restored and prepared for submission to PANDORA / TROVE, National Library of Australia, 2024.
